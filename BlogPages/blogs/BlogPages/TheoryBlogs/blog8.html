The reading explains and explores how information geographies have their own geographic distributions, such as geographies of access, of participation and of representation.

    One must understand that information has its own geography, if it is from somewhere or about somewhere then the information is evolved and transforms, it is then mediated by networks, infrastructure and technologies that all take up a physical space. This information is how we on the internet are able to learn information about places and will determine how we understand these different parts of the world (Graham et al., 2015, p.88). Information can be considered as a mobile as there is always the movement and transmission of information when moving from one place to another. There is are restrictions to keep information’s mutability (The likelihood of being changed) as defined by Latour (1986) who refers to “immutable mobiles” which is the ability to transfer information without significant change to its meaning or characteristics (Graham et al., 2015,p.88 – p89). The reading explains that the geography of information distribution represents a form of control and power. As can be seen with the distribution of access of different Geographies, as can be seen from data from 2011 China had been seen with the largest internet population with over half a billion users and were followed by India, the United states and Japan. There is however a large number of users who are unable to have access to the internet showing an unevenness in the geography of internet access but this can also be attributed towards Broadband affordability (Graham et al., 2015,p.91 – p93). To actually gauge the participation of different geographical locations there are three different variables this being domain names to indicate the different content on the Web, GitHub users as an indication of how many contributions there are too largest open software repository and Wikipedia edits. All these represent different communities of practice as can be seen within the illustrations displaying these numbers (Graham et al., 2015, p.94 – p97). .

    Geographies of representation is another point brought on by the discussion, to better understand the different representations within the (im)mutable augmentations of the world they had analysed the geographies of different digital representations such as Open Street Map, Google Search and the Semantic Web. Google searches can impact the representation of different places the study had analysed different countries search histories by name of the county itself. The United States had the largest number of mentions followed by Japan, China and The United Kingdom. These numbers were then displayed in chart of theirs displayed in the research. They then Mapped the Open Streen Map, what they had mapped was the different nodes of each cell this displaying how much content each country had displayed with all the nodes present in it, The United States again had the largest total amount, however Netherlands had the highest density of content. In terms of the Semantic Web they had analysed the total number of georeferenced objects within a country such as for example searches around asking “what is the capital of France”. The United States had contained the largest concentration of Freebase. This entire paper uses data to display the different geographies if access with addition to the uneven geographies of participation in this data, not ever country has the appropriate access to participate in these research analysed in the data displayed.
